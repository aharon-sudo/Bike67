# Bike67
Evolutionary AI that evolves UCI-legal bicycle frames from scratch using only the rulebook as a constraint. Independently discovered asymmetric wheel sizing in under an hour.

From Bikes to the World: A Framework for Rulebook-Constrained Evolutionary AI


Abstract:
This paper proposes a framework for applying evolutionary algorithms to constrained physical design problems, using competitive sport regulations as the sole boundary condition. The central argument is that human engineering, however sophisticated, is bounded not just by rules but by accumulated assumption. An AI system given only a regulatory document and a fitness function, with no inherited design knowledge, may find solutions that generations of human engineers have systematically missed. This paper documents both the theoretical framework and its first experimental results, produced in a single afternoon on a personal laptop at zero cost. The experiment used professional cycling's UCI regulations as the constraint set and produced a repeatable, statistically consistent finding: asymmetric wheel sizing is independently evolved by the algorithm across every run, converging on a smaller front wheel and larger rear wheel configuration that no current UCI road racing team deploys. The paper then outlines the scaling roadmap from cycling through Formula One, aerospace, and ultimately toward comprehensive systems simulation including human biological modeling.

1. The Question Behind Formula One
The initial inspiration for this framework came while listening to the Acquired podcast, which released a long-form episode in early 2026 covering Formula One as a business and engineering enterprise. The episode examines how F1 teams invest hundreds of millions annually chasing margins measured in hundredths of a second. Every team operates from the same FIA technical regulations. The rulebook is public. And yet performance gaps between teams are substantial and persistent.
The conventional explanation points to resources, talent, and institutional knowledge. But listening to that episode prompted a more interesting question: what if institutional knowledge is itself a constraint? What if the accumulated wisdom of decades of F1 engineering also carries accumulated assumptions, and those assumptions are quietly closing off areas of the design space that nobody is looking at?
The proposal that follows is straightforward. Feed the complete FIA technical regulations to an evolutionary AI system as the only design constraint. Begin with a minimal rule-compliant object, a structure that satisfies dimensional requirements and nothing more. Define a fitness function based on lap time performance inside a physics simulation. Let the system evolve through successive generations of mutation and selection, with the rulebook as the only hard boundary and no human design input in the loop.
The AI carries no prior knowledge of what a racing car should look like. It knows only what is legal and what performs. Given sufficient generations, it may converge on configurations that human engineers, shaped by everything they already know, have never explored.

2. The SpaceX Observation
A secondary line of thinking emerged while watching footage of successive Starship launches and noticing something that received little attention in mainstream coverage. Between vehicles, things had moved. Antenna arrays were repositioned. Pipe routing had changed. Small, unremarked physical differences that were not announced and not explained, but visible on close inspection.
The interpretation is that SpaceX is already running an evolutionary loop in hardware. Each launch is a generation. Performance and failure data feeds back into the next design iteration. The vehicle slowly improves not through a single breakthrough but through accumulated small changes selected by real-world fitness functions.
This is a meaningful proof of concept for the broader idea. Physical evolutionary iteration produces genuine engineering progress. The limitation in the SpaceX case, however, is that rocketry has no rulebook. The design space is vast and loosely bounded. Evolutionary pressure can work, but convergence is slow and expensive when the search space is nearly infinite and each generation costs hundreds of millions of dollars. Tighter constraints accelerate useful evolution. Which brings the argument back to sport.

3. Why Cycling Is the Right Starting Point
Formula One offers tighter constraints than rocketry, but it is still a highly complex system. Accurate simulation requires computational fluid dynamics infrastructure that is expensive and slow to iterate. The design space, while bounded by regulation, still spans aerodynamics, suspension geometry, power unit mapping, tire interaction, and dozens of other interdependent variables.
Reflecting on this, it became clear that the right first step was something even more constrained. Professional cycling, governed by UCI regulations, is that step. The rulebook is specific and narrow: frame geometry, minimum weight, wheel dimensions, rider position limits, handlebar reach and drop constraints, component restrictions. The physics of a bicycle moving through air on a road course are dramatically simpler to simulate than an F1 car at race speed. Generations that would take hours to evaluate in an F1 environment can be evaluated in seconds for a bicycle.
The methodology is identical. Parse the UCI regulations into a machine-readable constraint set. Begin with a minimal legal frame. Define a fitness function around total power required at 45 km/h, not just aerodynamic drag but rolling resistance and weight combined, which is the physically correct real-world fitness function. Evolve. If the system produces a bicycle geometry or configuration that human engineers have not tried, and that configuration proves faster, the core thesis is validated at low cost and high iteration speed.

4. Experimental Results
The following experiment was conducted on a personal laptop in a single afternoon using entirely open source tools: Python, the DEAP genetic algorithm library, OpenAI Gymnasium as the simulation wrapper, and the Martin et al. (1998) validated cycling power model as the physics engine. Total cost: zero dollars.
4.1 System Configuration
The genome consisted of 19 parameters covering head tube angle, seat tube angle, top tube length, head tube length, chainstay length, bottom bracket drop, fork offset, stack, reach, front and rear wheel diameter independently, four tube aspect ratios, frame weight, handlebar reach, handlebar drop, and handlebar width. Every candidate design was validated against the full UCI regulatory constraint set before evaluation. The initial population included box-geometry frames, maximally unoptimized designs satisfying only the dimensional minimums, ensuring the algorithm had to discover performance rather than start near it.
4.2 Key Finding: Independent Evolution of Asymmetric Wheel Sizing
Across every run conducted, regardless of random seed or generation count, the algorithm independently converged on asymmetric front and rear wheel sizes. Typical results showed a front wheel of approximately 550mm diameter and a rear wheel of 618 to 700mm diameter. This configuration was never suggested to the algorithm. It emerged purely from physics.
This finding has a historical precedent. In 1976, the Tyrrell Formula One team independently arrived at a six-wheel configuration featuring four smaller front wheels, reasoning that reduced frontal area would decrease aerodynamic drag while maintaining mechanical grip. The Tyrrell P34 won the 1976 Swedish Grand Prix. It was eventually banned not because it failed but because it worked in ways the regulations had not anticipated.
The evolutionary algorithm, given only UCI bicycle regulations and a power minimization objective, found an analogous insight: that matching wheel sizes is a human convention, not a physical optimum. The front wheel primarily needs to steer and present minimal frontal area. The rear wheel primarily needs to roll efficiently and maintain traction. These are different requirements, and different optimal sizes serve them. No current UCI road racing team races asymmetric wheel sizes. The algorithm found this configuration in under an hour.

4.4 Additional Geometric Findings
Beyond wheel sizing, the algorithm consistently converged on several geometry choices that human road bike designers apply inconsistently or not at all. All four main frame tubes were pushed to the maximum UCI-legal aero section ratio of 8:3 simultaneously. Human designers typically optimize one or two tubes and make compromises elsewhere. The algorithm applied maximum aero section everywhere with no compromise. Seat tube angles of 75 to 78 degrees emerged consistently, significantly steeper than the 72 to 74 degrees typical in road bike design. Stack to reach ratios significantly lower than standard road geometry produced an aggressive forward position that maximizes power output efficiency at the target speed.

5. The Scaling Roadmap
The cycling experiment validates the core methodology. The same framework now scales through increasing levels of complexity.
Cycling proves the concept in the simplest possible physical environment. The result is already in hand.
Formula One scales the approach to a vastly more complex system with a richer regulatory framework. The FIA technical regulations govern hundreds of parameters. An evolutionary AI starting from a minimal legal object with no F1 design heritage would search a space that decades of human engineering has never fully explored.
Rocketry extends the framework further, replacing a sport's rulebook with mission parameters and physical constraints as the boundary conditions. The SpaceX iterative loop already demonstrates that physical evolution works. Formalizing it with an AI-driven search would accelerate the process dramatically.
Full systems simulation represents the logical endpoint. A simulation environment comprehensive enough to model not just a vehicle but an entire system, economic, logistical, political, social, at sufficient fidelity that evolutionary AI can optimize strategies within it. The constraints become laws, regulations, physical constants, and behavioral models.
Human biological modeling is among the most compelling applications at this scale. A sufficiently detailed physiological model, one that accurately captures organ interaction, immune response, cellular behavior, and biochemical signaling, combined with this evolutionary framework, could transform drug discovery entirely. Rather than relying on human researchers to hypothesize which compounds might treat a given condition, the system would run millions of simulated interventions across generated variations of the model, selecting for outcomes. No prior assumption about what a drug should look like. No inherited bias about which pathways are worth targeting. The same principle that found asymmetric wheel sizing in a bicycle could, at sufficient scale and fidelity, find a treatment pathway that no researcher has thought to look for.

6. Discussion
The finding that an evolutionary algorithm independently rediscovers insights that human engineers arrived at through years of expensive experimentation suggests that the design spaces of constrained engineering problems contain recoverable structure. The optimum is findable. Human engineers find it slowly, expensively, and incompletely because they carry the weight of everything they already know.
The more significant implication is not what the algorithm found but what it might find that humans have not. The Tyrrell insight was eventually discovered by a human team. The more interesting question is whether there exist configurations in the UCI-legal bicycle design space, or the FIA-legal F1 car design space, that no human team has ever approached because the assumptions required to reach them are too far from the current consensus. The algorithm has no consensus. It has only constraints and a fitness function. That is both its limitation and its power.

7. Conclusion
The connecting thread across cycling, Formula One, rocketry, systems simulation, and human biology is a single idea: evolution works faster and finds more surprising solutions when the search space is well-bounded. Human engineers, researchers, and physicians all bound their search not just with rules but with everything they already believe. An AI system that begins with only the constraints, and nothing else, searches a different space entirely.
This paper began as a thought experiment prompted by a podcast episode about Formula One. It ended, on the same afternoon, with a working evolutionary system that independently rediscovered a design principle that won a Formula One race in 1976, applied now to bicycles, at zero cost, in a few hours.
The question is no longer whether this approach works. It is how far it scales, and how surprising the answers become at each level. The cheapest place to start was a bicycle. It worked. The next question is what happens when you hand it the FIA rulebook.
